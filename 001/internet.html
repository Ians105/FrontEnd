Definition

A global network of computers connected to each other that allows for communication through a standardized set of protocols.

The internet is not controlled by a person or entity. The internet is composed of many independently operated networks. While originally for resilient communication (ARPANET), the internet has evolved so that information can be shared with anyone. What may seem unimportant to you might be valuable to someone else.

Information Flow

Similar to how a postal service send packages from one location to another, the internet allows for the information to flow from one computer to another.

The information in the internet is in encoded in what we call bits, which are a binary representation of opposites like 1s and 0s. Every piece of data sent over the Internet, whether it's a message, image, or video, is first converted into bits. These bits are grouped into bytes, with one byte consisting of 8 bits. Bytes are the standard unit for measuring digital information in computing.

Units of Digital Information

As data increases in size, we use larger units to measure it. Below are some common units of digital information:

Unit Equals
1 Bit 1 Bit
1 Byte 8 Bits
1 Kilobyte (KB) 1,024 Bytes
1 Megabyte (MB) 1,024 Kilobytes

Physical Communication

In today's world, the bits can be sent through various physical mediums, such as electricity, light, and radio waves.

Problem

One problem with using physical mediums such as electricity and light is that how do we communicate several 1s or 0s in a row? A solution is to use a timer to determine how long the electricity or light is on or off.

Measure of Speed

  • Bandwidth is the maximum amount of transmission capacity of a communication medium. It is typically measured in bit rates, which is the number of bits that can be transmitted per second.
  • Latency is the measure of the time it takes for a bit to travel from one point to another. It is typically measured in milliseconds (ms).
